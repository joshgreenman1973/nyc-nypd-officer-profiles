The NYPD, officer by officer

In September 2025 the New York Police Department began publishing a profile of every active uniformed officer — rank, command, lifetime arrests, medals and sustained discipline — and refreshing it every week. This is that data, made searchable.

Point-in-time snapshot · exported — · refreshed weekly Built from the department's own Officer Profile datasets on NYC Open Data · Methodology & caveats
What time is this data for? It's a point-in-time snapshot the NYPD exported on —, and the department refreshes it every week. Every arrest and recognition figure is a cumulative career total as of that date — not a count for any single year. The force changes constantly as officers are hired and retire, so this is today's roster, not a historical record. The one place you can see genuine change over time is What changes over time, below.

Lifetime arrest and recognition totals are cumulative per officer, as reported by the department. "Sustained charge" means a disciplinary charge that ended in a guilty finding — see the caveat below.

What changes over time

Most of this data is a still photograph — arrest and award counts are lifetime totals with no yearly breakdown. But three date fields let us trace real change. Read the last year or two with care: they're incomplete, for the reasons noted under each chart. Hover any chart for the yearly figure.

Active officers by the year they were appointed. Earlier years are thinned by retirement, so this is the surviving cohort, not the size of each original class. The 2026 bar is a partial year.

Sustained charges, by year decided

Guilty disciplinary findings by the year they were decided. The most recent years lag sharply because cases are still moving through the process — not because misconduct stopped.

Top medals awarded, by year

The six highest valor and merit honors, held by officers on today's force, by the year the medal was awarded. Only medals belonging to still-active officers appear, so earlier years understate the totals.

The single most important caveat. The disciplinary data published here contains only charges that ended in a guilty finding — every one of the 3,925 charges has a disposition of guilty, pleaded guilty or no contest. Allegations that were dismissed, unsubstantiated or are still pending do not appear, and civilian complaints to the Civilian Complaint Review Board live in a separate system entirely. An officer with no record here has no sustained departmental charge — which is not the same as a clean complaint history.

How arrests break down

The department publishes a single lifetime arrest total per officer — no breakdown by charge, offense or year. But those totals are far from evenly spread. Here is how they vary across the force, by how many officers make them, by rank and by time served.

Detective grades dominate career totals. Switch to "per year of service" to strip out the fact that senior officers have simply had more years.

Arrests by time served

Career totals climb with tenure — mostly accumulation. The per-year rate shows officers are most active early, then ease off as they rise into supervisory roles.

An arrest credited here reflects an officer's assignment: patrol and detective commands make the most, while administrative, training and headquarters postings make few or none. Totals are cumulative over a career, so the per-year figures divide by years since appointment to compare activity on a level footing. There is no charge-level or offense-type detail in this data.

Methodology & caveats

Nothing here is a black box. Every figure comes from datasets the New York Police Department publishes on NYC Open Data and refreshes weekly. The site loads them directly; per-officer detail is fetched live from the same servers when you open an officer.

What to keep in mind

  • Discipline is guilty findings only. All 3,925 charges carry a guilty, pleaded-guilty or no-contest disposition. Dismissed, unsubstantiated and pending matters are absent, as are all Civilian Complaint Review Board complaints. Absence of a record here is not proof of a spotless history.
  • It's a live snapshot of active officers. Retired or separated members drop off entirely — this is not a historical roster of everyone who ever served. Medal-holders who have left the force are not counted among "the decorated."
  • Arrests and recognitions are pre-totalled. The department publishes lifetime cumulative counts, so they can't be sliced by year from this data alone.
  • The precinct map covers patrol precincts only. Officer commands were mapped to a precinct by parsing commands like 075 PRECINCT, with the Central Park precinct (the 22nd) matched by name. About 13,167 of 34,237 officers sit in a patrol precinct; the rest are in housing, transit, headquarters, the academy or specialized units and are not on the map. The boundary file contains 78 precinct areas.
  • Awards given vs officers who hold them. The same officer can receive an award many times, so the count of awards is far larger than the number of recipients — Excellent Police Duty is given 91,330 times but to 16,468 officers (about 5.5 each). The awards table shows both figures side by side. The medals, by contrast, are almost one-per-officer.
  • Two ways to count recognitions. The awards table and the "recognitions awarded" figure (141,235) count individual award records in the recognition dataset. The department also keeps a per-officer recognition counter whose values sum slightly higher, to 144,444; the difference is a quirk of how the two are maintained.
  • Training records contain known data-entry errors, per the department's own dataset description, so the training chart is best read as broad scale, not exact tallies.
  • Officer names are published by the department. This site republishes only what the NYPD already releases publicly, and adds no new personal information.

Reading the headcount

Total NYPD strength is a perennial fight at City Council budget hearings, and the raw 34,237 needs context before it is cited.

  • Recruits are in the count. 2,194 officers are in the "RTS RECRUITS" command — recruits in the Police Academy, and the single largest command on the force — plus about 126 in the Police Academy itself. They are counted as active members but are not deployed.
  • So are officers who aren't working. 776 sit at the "Military & Extended Leave Desk" — on military deployment or extended leave. Setting recruits and this group aside, the number available for duty is nearer 31,300 than 34,200.
  • Domestic-violence officers moved out of patrol. About 504 officers work in domestic-violence assignments, 455 of them in precinct-based "domestic violence squads" that are organizationally separate from the precinct patrol roster. They carry a precinct number but are counted apart from patrol strength — the work stayed local even as the officers were reorganized.
  • Patrol strength is not the same as a precinct's footprint. The precinct map counts patrol and field-training commands (13,167 officers). A further ~1,477 officers work in precinct-based detective and domestic-violence squads that fall outside that patrol count, so the map understates how many officers a precinct actually fields.

What time the data is for, and the trends

  • This is a weekly point-in-time snapshot, exported by the department on the date shown at the top of the page. Arrest and recognition figures are lifetime cumulative totals as of that date, with no annual breakdown — they are not counts for any single year.
  • The three "What changes over time" charts are the only genuine time series here, each built from a real date field: officer appointment_date, disciplinary charge date and award date.
  • Hiring by year is survivorship-biased. Because only active officers are in the data, earlier years show the surviving cohort, not the original class size — an officer hired in 1995 who has since retired isn't counted. The most recent year is partial.
  • Sustained charges by year lag at the recent end because cases take time to be adjudicated and published; a low recent count reflects the process, not a drop in conduct issues.
  • Medals by year understate earlier years, since only honors held by still-active officers appear.

How to read it

All counts start at zero. The awards bar chart uses a log scale because a handful of award types (Excellent and Meritorious Police Duty) dwarf the rest; every other chart is linear. The precinct choropleth uses a linear color ramp between the lowest and highest precinct values for the chosen metric.
